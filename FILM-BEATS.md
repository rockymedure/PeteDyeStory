# The Film — Beat by Beat

**20–25 minutes. Not a golf documentary. A family story.**

---

### 1. THE LAND

Black screen. A single bell tone — faint, distant, unexplained.

Then images: scarred hillsides. Orange water. Rusted mining equipment. An abandoned coal tipple. No people. No narration. Just the land as it was.

Jimmy's voice enters over the images:

> "My grandfather, James LaRosa — no middle name — came to this country in 1907."

Cut to James D. on camera. He's 91. Wood-paneled room. Looking straight at us.

> "I'm very glad that I love the country. I love the people."

We see Glen Elk — the brick buildings, the coal-town neighborhood where this family grew up.

---

### 2. THE DREAMER

Papa Jim. James D.'s son. A coal miner who became a builder. We see him on construction sites, in work clothes, pointing at land, dogs at his feet. This is a man who moves earth for a living.

Somewhere in the late '70s, he got a wild idea: build a golf course.

From the Grand Opening banquet:

> "This golf course is my thing in life, it's my labor of love."

---

### 3. THE LEGEND

Quick. TPC Sawgrass. Kiawah. Whistling Straits. The most famous golf courses in the world. All designed by one man.

Pete Dye on camera. White cap, glasses, white shirt. Standing on raw dirt with absolute authority.

The audience understands: this is the most important golf architect alive.

---

### 4. THE REJECTION

Pete at the Grand Opening banquet, telling the story himself. The audience is laughing.

> "Call it Miracle Mountain Golf Course — it'll be a miracle if anybody wanted to play it."

He told them no. The story should be over.

---

### 5. THE SECOND CHANCE

It isn't over. They found another piece of land — an abandoned coal mine on Simpson Creek. The same ground their family had worked.

They brought Pete back. He walked into the timber alone with a handful of surveyor flags. Two hours later he came out grinning.

> "I've worked on this concept in different places around the world, but I've never had a setting like this. I may never have a setting like this."

They shook hands on raw, torn-up ground. No contract. No lawyers.

**1978.**

---

### 6. THE LONG MIDDLE

This is the guts of the film. 18 years compressed.

**The partnership.** Two stubborn men who loved the same piece of ground. Pete burned the oaks Papa Jim wanted to save. Papa Jim moved the 18th green while Pete was away. Pete made him haul every yard back.

> "Am I your assistant, or are you my assistant?"
> "No, I'm your assistant, Jim. I'll always be your assistant."

**The crises.** Papa Jim went into the mine shaft on #13 to check it. Black damp. Ten minutes unconscious. Most miners don't survive that.

They refinanced three times. Three near-foreclosures.

March 1993 — the Storm of the Century. Thirty inches of snow. The back nine pushed an entire year.

The lake on #15 leaked. They drained it, fixed it, refilled it. It leaked again.

The Lupton loan — Jimmy, in his early 30s, asking a stranger for a million dollars. He said yes. Then his office called and said he'd changed his mind. They wrote a letter. The CFO called back: "I don't know what you put in that letter, but Mr. Lupton reversed his decision. He's never done that before."

**The team.** These weren't corporate contractors. They built bunkers by hand. Gary Grandstaff stayed 30 years. Louie Ellis filmed for 20 years and never sent a bill.

> "Like I said 14 years ago, no charge. And there's no charge today."

**Alice.** Pete's wife walked the course once. "Pete, if this is designed to be a men's club, you won't finish the course." Pete: "OK, Allie." She changed four holes in one visit.

**1978... 1982... 1985... 1989... 1993.**

---

### 7. THE NAMING

Pete and Papa Jim on the 6th fairway. A quiet moment.

Jimmy tells the story:

> "Pete said — the hell with it. Let's call it Pete Dye."

Then, the line that changes everything:

> "I want you to know — this will be the only club named after me."

Not Sawgrass. Not Kiawah. Not Whistling Straits. Not the Ocean Course. He named none of them after himself.

He named a coal mine in West Virginia.

Faint bell tone.

---

### 8. OPENING DAY

**July 4, 1993.** Front nine opens. Only nine holes ready. Two bunkers without sand. Rain the night before. Bagpipes down the first fairway.

**1994.** Back nine opens.

**July 4, 1995.** Grand Opening. Pete and Alice. Joe DiMaggio. Two governors. 18 years, 18 holes.

Papa Jim at the banquet:

> "This golf course is my thing in life, it's my labor of love, and I don't think that I could have shared it with anybody else in this manner than I have with you."

---

### 9. THE BELL

The bell on the 12th hole. Close-up. Morning light.

Jimmy explains: the bell was installed in memory of Pete's father — the man who built Pete's first nine holes at Urbana Country Club.

Three fathers. Three sons.

- James D. came to America in 1907. His son Papa Jim dreamed of building a golf course on the land his family had worked.
- Papa Jim and his son Jimmy spent 18 years refusing to quit.
- Pete Dye's father built Pete's first nine holes.

On the morning of the Grand Opening, Pete saw the bell for the first time.

He rang it.

The bell — full, resonant. Hold.

---

### 10. THE PROOF

Quick. Title cards, magazine covers:

- Golf Digest: Top 100 in America
- Golfweek: #2 among 9,000 modern courses
- 474 members. 27 states. 5 countries.
- Ranked alongside Augusta and Pine Valley

The proof isn't the point. The proof is what makes the improbability undeniable.

---

### 11. THE LAND

Drone. Sunrise. Pulling back over the full 18 holes. The same ground that opened the film scarred and abandoned — now alive.

> "There is nothing more dramatic than this piece of property. You can't get 18 holes any better than this. Take my word for it."

Bell sound — one final ring. Echoing over the hills.

**White text on black screen:**

*Pete Dye designed over 100 golf courses.*
*He put his name on one.*

---

*20–25 minutes. The footage exists. The story is real.*
