## A Documentary Film About the Pete Dye Golf Club

## The Story

Two coal miners. Father and son. An Italian immigrant's son and grandson from the hills of West Virginia. They had a dream to build a golf course, so they went to the most famous golf course architect on earth — the man behind Sawgrass, Kiawah, Whistling Straits — and he told them to forget it.

They didn't.

They found an abandoned coal mine — the same land their family had worked for generations — and brought him back. He walked it alone for two hours, came out grinning, and said he could build one of the finest inland courses in the country. They shook hands. They didn't even own most of the land yet.

It took 18 years. Three near-foreclosures. Thirty inches of snow. A father who nearly died in a mine shaft. A lake that leaked twice. A million-dollar loan that got approved, rescinded, and reinstated because of a letter.

And somewhere along the way, Pete Dye — who'd designed over 100 courses and never named one after himself — put his name on this one. Not Sawgrass. Not Kiawah. Not Whistling Straits. A coal mine in West Virginia, built by two guys who kept refinancing just to keep the lights on.

On the morning of the Grand Opening, he rang a bell they'd hung on the 12th hole in memory of his father. The man who built Pete's first nine holes.

Two coal miners built something the world said couldn't exist. The greatest architect alive staked his legacy on it. And it became one of the best golf courses in America — Top 100 in the country, #2 among 9,000 modern courses, ranked alongside Augusta and Pine Valley. 474 members from 27 states and 5 countries. The family is still there, five generations deep. The bell is still on the 12th hole. They still ask you to ring it.

*Pete Dye designed over 100 golf courses. He put his name on one.*

## Why This Film Matters to West Virginia

West Virginia has spent decades trying to change its national story. The prevailing narrative — coal decline, population loss, economic hardship — defines how the rest of the country sees the state. Every year, millions are spent on tourism campaigns and economic development marketing to counter that perception.

This film is the counter-narrative. And it already exists.

### 1. It reframes what West Virginia means.

The national conversation about WV is about what the state has lost. This film is about what it built. Not in spite of the coal heritage — *because of it*. The same land, the same families, the same stubbornness that powered the coal industry produced something world-class. Pete Dye didn't hide the mining scars. He insisted they stay. The tipple. The mine openings. The coal car at the gate. The course is a living museum of what West Virginia was *and* what it became.

That's not a golf message. That's an identity message. And it's one the state has never had a vehicle this powerful to deliver.

### 2. It's destination marketing that doesn't feel like marketing.

Every person who watches this film wants to see the place. Not because someone told them West Virginia is beautiful — because they watched two men spend 18 years building something impossible in those hills and they want to stand on that ground.

The course already draws members from 27 states and 5 countries. The film turns that into a broader pull — not just for golfers, but for anyone who responds to a story about perseverance, family, and place.

The tourism play: don't sell the scenery. Sell the story. The scenery sells itself once people arrive.

### 3. It reaches an audience West Virginia tourism doesn't normally reach.

Golf is an $84 billion industry. Pete Dye is one of the five most recognizable names in course design worldwide. This film, distributed through golf media channels, festival circuits, and streaming platforms, reaches an affluent, travel-oriented demographic that standard tourism campaigns rarely touch. These are people who plan trips around courses. They fly to Scotland, to Bandon Dunes, to Kiawah. This film puts West Virginia on that map.
