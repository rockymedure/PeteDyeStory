# Edit Guide: Pete Dye Golf Club Documentary
## Scene-by-Scene Blueprint for the Editor

> **See also: [FOR-SETH-REVISION-MAP.md](FOR-SETH-REVISION-MAP.md)** — Maps Seth's existing rough cut (timecodes 00:00–28:55) directly to this scene structure. Start there if you're working from the current "Heaven in the Hills" cut.

**Target Runtime:** 20–25 minutes  
**Format:** Documentary (interview + archival + drone)  
**Tone:** Earned, not sentimental. Let the improbability of the story do the emotional work.  
**Core Question:** How did two coal miners from West Virginia convince the greatest golf architect in the world to stake his legacy on an abandoned strip mine — and then spend 18 years proving he was right to do it?

---

## SONIC MOTIF: THE BELL

A single bell tone — the bell on #12, installed in memory of Pete Dye's father — should appear three times:

1. **Faintly, under the opening image** (before we know what it is)
2. **At the naming scene** (a subtle presence as the stakes become clear)
3. **As the final sound of the film** (full, resonant, the emotional release)

The bell is the thread that ties three father-son stories together: James D. and Papa Jim, Papa Jim and Jimmy, Pete and his father.

## SCENE 1: THE LAND AS IT WAS
**Runtime:** 1:30–2:00  
**Purpose:** Make the audience think nothing good could ever come from this place.

**Source material:**
- `Joung JJL Coal Mining Footage No Sound` — Historic mining footage, coal trains, tipple, scarred hillsides
- `Aug 1985 PDGC Construction Cleaning out Simpson Creek` — Iron-stained water, rusted equipment, derelict mine openings
- `Some Highlites Aug 1982-1988` — Early raw site footage before any shaping
- `Papa Jim Interview` — Glen Elk neighborhood tour (Chapter 1–2), showing the coal-era buildings, the declining neighborhood, the world these families came from

**What I want to see:**  
No narration at first. Just images. The scarred land. The orange water. The rusted machinery. The coal tipple. Let the audience sit with what this place was.

Then, faintly, the bell tone.

Then Jimmy's voice — from his modern interview — over the images:

> "My grandfather, James LaRosa — no middle name — fought in both the Italian and American armies, came here, met my grandmother on the property..."

**Cut to:** James D. on camera, at 91, giving his life story:

> "I came to this country in 1907... I'm very glad that I love the country, I love the people."

**The point:** Before this is a golf story, it's an immigration story. The family's roots are in the coal under this land. The audience needs to feel that bone-deep before we introduce Pete Dye or mention golf.

## SCENE 2: THE DREAMER
**Runtime:** 1:30–2:00  
**Purpose:** Establish Papa Jim — James D.'s son, coal miner, builder, dreamer.

**Source material:**
- `Papa Jim Interview` — Glen Elk tour pointing out where James D. was born; the blasting/commercial development sequence (Chapter 4) where Jimmy describes the 3-year hill project
- `PDGC Grand Opening` (banquet) — Papa Jim calling the course "my labor of love" and "my thing in life" [~00:30:00]
- Any archival footage of Papa Jim on the construction site in work clothes, pointing, directing equipment, with his dogs

**What I want to see:**  
A quick portrait of Papa Jim: the buildings he built, the land he moved, his energy. The audience should understand — this man moved earth for a living. He was a coal miner's son who became a builder. And somewhere in the late '70s, he got a wild idea: build a golf course.

From the Grand Opening banquet, Papa Jim:

> "This golf course is my thing in life, it's my labor of love, and I don't think that I could have shared it with anybody else in this manner than I have with you."

Hold on his face. The audience should feel how much this man cared.

## SCENE 3: WHO IS PETE DYE?
**Runtime:** 0:45–1:00  
**Purpose:** Establish Pete's stature so the rejection and later naming carry weight.

**Source material:**
- `The Pete Dye Golf Course Narrated by Harris Holt` — Professional narration about Pete's career and design philosophy
- `Interview With Pete Dye 7-10-94` — Pete on design evolution, citing Pinehurst, Harbour Town, Crooked Stick, The Honors Course [Chapter 3, ~00:05:00–00:07:30]
- Stock or reference photos of TPC Sawgrass, Ocean Course at Kiawah, Whistling Straits, Teeth of the Dog (if rights allow, even 5-second clips or magazine covers)
- `PDGC Grand Opening` (banquet) — Pete's line about competitors: "I've been here over 17 years. I think if I make it back another time, I'll make 400 trips. That's about the total that some of my competitors make in their whole lifetime on all their jobs." [~00:27:40]

**What I want to see:**  
Quick, authoritative. Not a bio — a flash. TPC Sawgrass island green. The Ryder Cup at Kiawah. Whistling Straits. Harris Holt's voice naming the courses. Then Pete on camera from the 1994 interview, talking about design with absolute command.

The audience should think: *this is the most important golf architect alive.*

Then cut hard to —

## SCENE 4: THE REJECTION
**Runtime:** 1:00–1:30  
**Purpose:** The story should be over. Pete says no.

**Source material:**
- `PDGC Grand Opening` (banquet) — Pete retells the Miracle Mountain story live [Chapter 3, ~00:09:30–00:12:00]. He and Jimmy trade versions. Pete: "Call it Miracle Mountain Golf Course — it'll be a miracle if anybody wanted to play it." Jimmy explains they briefly hired Gary Player's firm but couldn't shake Pete's honesty.
- `Interview With Pete Dye 7-10-94` — Pete's broader commentary about the first visit

**What I want to see:**  
Pete telling the story himself, from the banquet. His voice. His delivery. The audience laughing, but also understanding: this is a man who doesn't waste his time on mediocrity. He just told two coal miners from West Virginia that their dream isn't worth his name.

Jimmy's modern interview: the sting of the rejection. But also the stubbornness — they didn't stop looking.

## SCENE 5: THE SECOND CHANCE
**Runtime:** 1:30–2:00  
**Purpose:** They find the mine. Pete sees something no one else can.

**Source material:**
- `PDGC Grand Opening` (banquet) — Jimmy and Papa Jim's telling of finding the Simpson Creek property on USGS maps, bringing Pete back [Chapter 3 continued; Chapter 6, ~00:27:00–00:31:30]
- Early construction footage (any tape from 1982-1988) showing the raw site — gob piles, tipple, derelict machinery
- `Papa Jim Interview` — Pete on site praising the land [Chapter 11, ~01:12:30]: "I felt like I've worked on this concept in different places around the world, but I've never had a setting like this. I may never have a setting like this."
- `PDGC Grand Opening` (banquet) — Pete: "When I walked in here and saw this ground, yes, it was all torn up from the coal, but you could see the routing going down and coming back... this will never happen again, has never happened before, will never happen again to me." [~00:36:40]
- Archival photos of Pete walking with flags (referenced in Substack and rough cut analysis)

**What I want to see:**  
The contrast. Cut between the scarred, ruined land and Pete's words about what he saw in it. He walked into the timber with flags and came out two hours later with 18 holes in his head.

The handshake. Jimmy's modern interview:

> "It was a handshake. A little bit surreal because I'm not sure at that time I was really aware that the Pete Dye Golf Club was going to just spread like wildfire."

The audience should feel: something improbable is about to be attempted by people who have no business attempting it.

**First year super appears on screen: 1978**

## SCENE 6: THE LONG MIDDLE — 18 YEARS
**Runtime:** 5:00–7:00  
**Purpose:** This is the guts of the film. Show — don't tell — why it took 18 years, and what these men endured. This is where the rough cut is weakest and where the real film lives.

This scene has **sub-sections** that intercut with each other. The year supers keep advancing. Construction footage provides the visual spine.

### 6A: The Partnership
**Source material:**
- `PDGC Grand Opening` (banquet) — Pete/Papa Jim banter. The oaks on #2 story [Chapter 4/7, ~00:15:30–00:22:30]. The 18th hole debacle [Chapter 3, ~00:12:00]. Pete: "I never met a man that can't play golf that loves golf as much as Jim LaRosa." [~00:15:10]
- `Interview With Pete Dye 7-10-94` — Pete: "Am I your assistant, or are you my assistant?" Papa Jim: "No, I'm your assistant, Jim. I'll always be your assistant." [~00:10:05]
- Any archival of Pete and Papa Jim together on the dirt — walking, arguing, pointing, laughing

**What I want to see:**  
The relationship. Two stubborn men who loved the same piece of ground. Pete burned the oaks Papa Jim wanted to save. Papa Jim moved the 18th green while Pete was away. Pete made him haul every yard back. And through it all:

> "Papa Jim was the toughest, most tenacious, never-give-up son of a gun I ever worked for."

**Year super: 1982**

### 6B: The Crises
**Source material:**
- Construction tapes from 1985–1994 (any footage showing weather damage, flooding, bare dirt, stalled progress)
- `PDGC Construction 8-31-93 thru 7-7-94` — Final push footage
- Jimmy's existing interviews and banquet-tape audio telling the specific stories — black damp on #13, the Storm of the Century, the lake on #15 leaking twice, the near-foreclosures, the Lupton million-dollar loan. Search all processed transcripts for these references. If specific crisis narration doesn't exist in the archive, reconstruct visually with AI-generated footage over existing VO.

**What I want to see:**  
Jimmy's voice over construction footage. Not generalities. Specifics.

- "Papa Jim went into the mine shaft on #13 to inspect it. He forgot the golden rule — never enter without ventilation. He collapsed almost instantly. Black damp. Ten minutes unconscious. Most miners don't survive that."
- "We refinanced at least three times. On at least three occasions it came close to foreclosure."
- "March 1993. The Storm of the Century. Thirty inches of snow. Then another two feet. The back nine got pushed an entire year."
- "The lake on fifteen leaked. We drained it, repaired it, refilled it. It leaked again."
- The Lupton loan: "I was in my early 30s, asking a man I'd never met for a million dollars. He said yes. Then his office called and said he'd changed his mind. We wrote a letter. His CFO called back: 'I don't know what you put in that letter, but Mr. Lupton reversed his decision. He's never done that before.'"

Each crisis is 20-40 seconds. Quick. Stacking. The audience should feel the accumulation of setbacks.

**Year supers advancing: 1985... 1987... 1989... 1991... 1993**

### 6C: The Team
**Source material:**
- `Interview With Pete Dye 7-10-94` — Jimmy's roll call of staff [Chapter 5, ~00:08:30–00:10:00]: Bill Stines, Gary Grandstaff, Keith "Sparky" Sparkman, Mike Langkau, Jim Jamieson
- `Papa Jim Interview` — Gary Grandstaff's technical walkthrough, the hand-raking of greens, the craftsmanship [Chapters 6, 8, 10, 12]
- Any footage of the crew working with their hands — raking, shoveling, building bunkers, carrying sod up ladders on steep slopes

**What I want to see:**  
30-45 seconds. These weren't corporate contractors. They built bunkers by hand. A scoop of sand here, a bucket of dirt there. Pete sent his best shapers — the same men who shaped the Ocean Course and Teeth of the Dog. Gary Grandstaff stayed 30 years. Louie Ellis filmed for 20 years and never sent a bill.

Louie's sign-off from the 1994 interview:

> "Like I said 14 years ago, no charge. And there's no charge today."

### 6D: Alice
**Source material:**
- `PDGC Grand Opening` (banquet) — Alice is honored; her critique of the 17th: "Would you like it if somebody else had built it?" [referenced in analysis]
- Any archival photo of Alice with Pete on site
- Jimmy's modern interview or banquet tape for Alice's first visit. Her line: "Pete, if this is designed to be a men's club, you won't finish the course." Pete: "OK, Allie." Search existing transcripts for any reference to Alice.

**What I want to see:**  
20-30 seconds. One photo of Alice. Jimmy telling the story. Her impact was immediate — forward tees on 10 and 11, the 12th green mound moved, the 17th softened. She changed the course in one visit.

## SCENE 7: THE NAMING
**Runtime:** 2:00–2:30  
**Purpose:** The turn of the film. Pete stakes his legacy on this project. Of 100+ courses, this is the only one.

**Source material:**
- Jimmy's modern interview (already in rough cut, ~17:30–20:00) — The 6th fairway story. Pete and Papa Jim on the fairway. "The hell with it. Let's call it Pete Dye."
- `PDGC Grand Opening` (banquet) — Any additional telling of this moment
- Course footage of the 6th hole
- The clubhouse inscription: "18 of the most exciting and memorable holes I have ever built on one course"

**What I want to see:**  
Jimmy's retelling is the emotional centerpiece. He's already done this well in the rough cut. Keep it. But add what's missing:

> "I want you to know this will be the only club named after me."

If that line is in the existing interview, use it. If not, it needs to come from the banquet tape or a title card.

The audience should understand the weight. TPC Sawgrass. The Ocean Course. Whistling Straits. Teeth of the Dog. He named *none* of them after himself. He named this one. A coal mine in West Virginia built by two miners who kept calling him back.

Second bell tone — subtle, under the music.

## SCENE 8: OPENING DAY
**Runtime:** 1:30–2:00  
**Purpose:** After 15-18 years, it's real.

**Source material:**
- `PDGC Opening 7-3-1993` — Front nine opening, July 4, 1993
- `PDGC 1994 Opening of the back nine Bember-Guest` — Back nine completion
- `PDGC Grand Opening` — 1995 Grand Opening banquet footage, tent, speeches, the evening
- Rough cut footage of bagpipes, ceremony B-roll, fireworks

**What I want to see:**  
A clear timeline with date supers:

- **July 4, 1993** — Front nine opens. Only nine holes ready. Two bunkers without sand. Rain the night before. Leigh unpacking boxes with two toddlers. Bagpipes down the first fairway.
- **1994** — Back nine opens.
- **July 4, 1995** — Grand Opening. Pete and Alice there. Joe DiMaggio. Two governors. Mark Shaw. 18 years, 18 holes.

From the rough cut, the bagpipe footage is powerful. Keep it. But give it a date. Give it context. The audience should know: Papa Jim worked for 15 years before a single golfer played a hole.

## SCENE 9: THE BELL
**Runtime:** 1:00–1:30  
**Purpose:** The emotional climax of the film.

**Source material:**
- `PDGC Grand Opening` — **SEARCH FOR:** footage of Pete at #12 on the morning of July 4, 1995, seeing and ringing the bell. This may be in an unanalyzed section of the tape or in a different opening-day tape.
- If archival footage doesn't exist, reconstruct the moment visually using AI-assisted video generation — a faithful recreation matching the archival look and feel: morning light, the bell in close-up, a hand reaching for it, the ring. Layer Jimmy's existing interview audio or banquet-tape narration over the generated imagery. The bell sound itself can be sourced from any existing tape or generated clean for the motif.

**What I want to see:**  
The father-son thread made explicit. Three fathers. Three sons.

- James D. LaRosa came to America in 1907. His son Papa Jim dreamed of building a golf course on the land his family had worked.
- Papa Jim and his son Jimmy spent 18 years refusing to quit.
- Pete Dye's father built Pete's first nine holes. A bell on the 12th hole honors that man's memory.

On the morning of the Grand Opening, Pete saw the bell for the first time. He rang it.

This is the climax. Not a ranking. Not an accolade. A man ringing a bell for his father, on a course he spent 18 years building with another father and son.

Third bell tone — full, resonant. Hold it.

**What I want to see:**  
Drone pulling back. The full course visible — the hills, Simpson Creek, the holes carved from coal country. The same land that opened the film scarred and abandoned, now alive.

Bell sound — one final ring. Clean. Echoing over the hills.

**Title card (white text, black screen):**

*Pete Dye designed over 100 golf courses.*  
*He put his name on one.*

## WHAT THIS IS NOT

This is not a golf documentary. Do not open with course beauty shots. Do not lead with rankings. Do not structure it as "here's a great golf course, here's how it was built." The audience for this film includes people who have never played golf and never will.

This is a story about two coal miners who had a dream that was bigger than anyone thought possible — including the greatest architect in the game — and then spent 18 years refusing to let it die.

The golf is the proof. The story is the people.
