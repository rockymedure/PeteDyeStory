# Revision Map: Seth's Rough Cut → The Film We're Making

**Purpose:** This is the bridge document. It takes Seth's existing 28:55 cut of "Heaven in the Hills" and maps every minute to the stronger story structure. For each scene in the target film, it tells Seth exactly: keep this, move that, cut this, add this from archival.

**The principle:** We're not starting over. About 65-70% of Seth's cut is the right footage in roughly the right place. The other 30% is restructuring, supplementing from the 180+ pre-extracted archival clips, and — critically — adding the emotional climax that the current cut is missing entirely.

**Target runtime:** 20–25 minutes  
**Current runtime:** 28:55  
**Net change:** Cut ~8 minutes of repetition, add ~4-5 minutes of new material = ~25 min final

---

## THE CORE PROBLEM WITH THE CURRENT CUT

Seth's cut tells a real story. The heritage material is excellent, Pete naming the club is genuinely moving, and the closing lands. But it functions as a compressed super-teaser — all three acts squeezed into 29 minutes — rather than a film with a clear arc that builds to a climax.

Three structural issues:

1. **Act II is hollow.** 12:30–17:30 covers 18 years in 5 minutes with zero specific crises. We hear "every conceivable challenge" but never see one. The audience is told these men persevered but given no evidence.

2. **The emotional climax is missing.** The bell on #12 — Pete ringing it on Grand Opening morning in memory of his father — is the intended apex of the entire film. It's not in the cut. Not visually, not verbally.

3. **Act III material is pre-spent.** Hospitality (20:00–22:30) and "magic at the gates" (22:30–25:00) are payoff beats that belong after Opening Day. They appear in the middle of the cut, robbing Act III of its reward energy.

## WHAT THIS IS NOT

This is not a golf documentary. Do not open with course beauty shots. Do not lead with rankings. The audience for this film includes people who have never played golf and never will.

This is a story about two coal miners who had a dream that was bigger than anyone thought possible — including the greatest architect in the game — and then spent 18 years refusing to let it die.

The golf is the proof. The story is the people.

This is why it matters to West Virginia. This is why sponsors will care. Not because it's a great golf course — because it's the most powerful story of working-class perseverance, immigrant heritage, and generational legacy the state has ever produced. And it happens to have been validated by the entire golf world.

What we need is the story of how it got there. If someone who's never heard of Pete Dye watches this film, they should be able to tell you exactly what happened, in order, by the time it's over.

Your footage is in this film. The beauty is in this film. But it comes at the end — as the reward — not at the beginning.

Everything else you've done is the foundation. This restructure turns a strong portrait into the definitive story.
